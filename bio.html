Biology: Genetics

Genetics is the study of heredity, focusing on how traits are passed from parents to offspring. It involves understanding DNA, genes, and the various patterns of inheritance that govern traits in living organisms.

Basic Genetic Concepts

Genes are segments of DNA that code for proteins, which determine an organism's traits. Each individual inherits two alleles for each geneâ€”one from each parent. The combination of these alleles influences characteristics such as eye color, height, and susceptibility to diseases.

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is structured as a double helix, composed of nucleotides, each containing a sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base (adenine, thymine, cytosine, or guanine). The sequence of these bases forms the genetic code.

Dominant and Recessive Traits

Some traits follow simple Mendelian inheritance patterns, named after Gregor Mendel, the father of genetics. He discovered that some traits are dominant, meaning they are expressed if at least one dominant allele is present. Recessive traits, on the other hand, require two copies of the recessive allele to be expressed.

  • Example: In pea plants, the allele for tall height (T) is dominant over the allele for short height (t). A plant with TT or Tt will be tall, while only a plant with tt will be short.
  • Example: In humans, the allele for brown eyes (B) is dominant over the allele for blue eyes (b), meaning a person with BB or Bb will have brown eyes, while only a person with bb will have blue eyes.

Complex Inheritance Patterns

Not all traits follow simple dominant-recessive patterns. More complex forms of inheritance include:

  • Incomplete Dominance: A blending of traits occurs when neither allele is completely dominant. Example: A red-flowered plant (RR) crossed with a white-flowered plant (WW) produces pink-flowered offspring (RW).
  • Codominance: Both alleles are expressed equally. Example: In blood types, a person with the IA and IB alleles has type AB blood, showing both A and B antigens.
  • Multiple Alleles: Some genes have more than two possible alleles. Example: The ABO blood group system includes three alleles: IA, IB, and i.
  • Polygenic Traits: Traits controlled by multiple genes, leading to a range of phenotypes. Example: Human skin color and height are determined by several genes interacting together.

Sex-Linked Inheritance

Some genes are located on the sex chromosomes (X and Y), leading to sex-linked inheritance. Since males have only one X chromosome (XY), they are more likely to express X-linked recessive traits.

  • Example: Hemophilia, a blood clotting disorder, is more common in males because they only need one affected X chromosome to express the condition.
  • Example: Color blindness is also more common in males due to its X-linked recessive inheritance pattern.

Genetic Disorders and Mutations

Mutations are changes in DNA that can lead to genetic disorders. Some disorders follow Mendelian inheritance, while others result from chromosomal abnormalities.

  • Sickle Cell Anemia: A codominant disorder where abnormal hemoglobin leads to sickle-shaped red blood cells.
  • Down Syndrome: Caused by an extra copy of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21).
  • Cystic Fibrosis: A recessive disorder affecting the lungs and digestive system.
  • Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy: A sex-linked disorder that causes muscle degeneration and weakness.

Genetic Technologies and Ethics

Advancements in genetic research have led to breakthroughs in medical science. Technologies such as CRISPR allow scientists to edit genes with precision, potentially curing genetic diseases. However, ethical concerns arise regarding genetic modification, cloning, and designer babies.

Understanding genetics allows scientists to develop treatments, predict genetic disorders, and explore new possibilities in medicine and biotechnology.

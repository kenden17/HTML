Unit 2: Population and Migration

This unit explores population patterns, demographic data, and the movement of people. It analyzes how population growth, distribution, and migration impact society, economics, and the environment.

1. Population Distribution

  • Arithmetic Density: Total population divided by total land area.
  • Physiological Density: Population divided by arable (farmable) land.
  • Agricultural Density: Number of farmers per unit of arable land.
  • Population Clusters: Most people live in East Asia, South Asia, Europe, and Southeast Asia.

2. Population Growth

  • Crude Birth Rate (CBR): Number of births per 1,000 people annually.
  • Crude Death Rate (CDR): Number of deaths per 1,000 people annually.
  • Natural Increase Rate (NIR): CBR minus CDR, indicating population growth.
  • Total Fertility Rate (TFR): Average number of children per woman.
  • Doubling Time: Time needed to double a population at current growth rate.

3. Demographic Transition Model (DTM)

The DTM describes population changes over time in four (sometimes five) stages:

  1. Stage 1: High birth and death rates, no growth.
  2. Stage 2: Death rate falls, birth rate remains high, rapid growth.
  3. Stage 3: Birth rate declines, growth slows.
  4. Stage 4: Low birth and death rates, stable or slow growth.
  5. Stage 5 (optional): Birth rates fall below death rates, population decline.

4. Population Pyramids and Aging

Population pyramids visually represent age and gender structure. Wide bases indicate growth, while narrow ones show decline. Aging populations challenge economies and healthcare systems.

5. Population Policies

  • Pro-natalist Policies: Encourage population growth (e.g., France).
  • Anti-natalist Policies: Reduce birth rates (e.g., China’s One-Child Policy).

6. Migration

  • Push Factors: Conditions that drive people away (e.g., war, famine).
  • Pull Factors: Conditions that attract people (e.g., jobs, safety).
  • Voluntary Migration: Chosen movement, often for economic reasons.
  • Forced Migration: Involuntary movement due to conflict, persecution, or disaster.

7. Migration Patterns and Theories

  • Internal Migration: Movement within a country (e.g., rural to urban).
  • International Migration: Movement across country borders.
  • Chain Migration: Migration that follows family or community connections.
  • Step Migration: A series of shorter, less extreme migrations to a final destination.
  • Ravenstein’s Laws of Migration: General patterns of migration, including short-distance dominance and urban destination.
  • Gravity Model: Predicts migration based on population size and distance between locations.

8. Effects of Migration

  • Economic: Migrants often fill labor gaps and contribute to growth.
  • Cultural: Migration spreads language, religion, and customs.
  • Political: Can lead to debates about immigration policy and integration.

Summary

Unit 2 examines how population characteristics and migration shape societies. Understanding demographic trends and the causes and consequences of migration helps geographers and policymakers address challenges related to population growth, movement, and diversity.

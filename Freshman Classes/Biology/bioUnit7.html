Unit 7: Evolution

Evolution is the process by which populations of organisms change over generations through variations in traits and natural selection. This unit explores the evidence for evolution, mechanisms that drive it, and the contributions of key scientists like Charles Darwin.

1. Early Ideas About Evolution

Before Darwin, scientists like Lamarck proposed that organisms could change during their lifetimes and pass those traits on. However, Lamarck's idea of acquired traits was later disproven.

Charles Darwin introduced the concept of natural selection after observing species, especially during his travels to the Galápagos Islands. His book, On the Origin of Species, provided strong evidence for evolution.

2. Natural Selection

Natural selection is the process where individuals with favorable traits are more likely to survive and reproduce. Over time, these traits become more common in the population.

  • Variation: Individuals in a population vary in their traits.
  • Overproduction: More offspring are produced than can survive.
  • Competition: Offspring compete for limited resources.
  • Adaptation: Traits that improve survival and reproduction become more common.

3. Evidence of Evolution

  • Fossil Record: Shows changes in species over time and transitional forms.
  • Comparative Anatomy: Similar structures (homologous) suggest common ancestry.
  • Embryology: Early development of organisms shows shared traits.
  • Molecular Biology: DNA and protein similarities show genetic relationships.
  • Biogeography: Geographic distribution of species supports evolutionary history.

4. Mechanisms of Evolution

  • Mutation: Random changes in DNA can introduce new traits.
  • Gene Flow: Movement of genes between populations.
  • Genetic Drift: Random changes in allele frequency, especially in small populations.
  • Natural Selection: Non-random increase of advantageous traits.
  • Sexual Selection: Traits that improve mating success become more common.

5. Speciation

Speciation is the formation of new species. It occurs when populations become isolated and evolve separately.

  • Geographic Isolation: Physical barriers prevent populations from interbreeding.
  • Reproductive Isolation: Differences in mating behavior or timing prevent breeding.
  • Adaptive Radiation: One species evolves into many different forms to adapt to various environments.

6. Evolution in Action

Evolution is observable today in examples like antibiotic resistance in bacteria or changes in animal traits due to environmental pressure.

  • Peppered Moth: Color change due to industrial pollution.
  • Darwin’s Finches: Beak size changes based on food availability.
  • MRSA Bacteria: Resistance to antibiotics due to rapid evolution.

7. Misconceptions About Evolution

  • "Evolution is just a theory": In science, a theory is a well-tested explanation with strong evidence.
  • "Individuals evolve": Evolution occurs in populations, not individuals.
  • "Evolution has a goal": Evolution does not aim for perfection—it's driven by survival and reproduction.

Summary

Evolution explains the diversity of life on Earth. Through mechanisms like natural selection, mutation, and genetic drift, populations change over time. Evidence from fossils, DNA, and anatomy supports the theory of evolution. Understanding evolution helps us explain how species adapt and survive in a changing world.

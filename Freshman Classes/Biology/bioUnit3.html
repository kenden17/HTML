Unit 3: Cell Structure and Function

Cells are the basic units of life. This unit explores the cell theory, differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, the structure and function of cell organelles, and how cells maintain homeostasis through membrane transport.

1. The Cell Theory

The cell theory is a fundamental principle in biology, stating that:

  • All living things are made of one or more cells.
  • Cells are the basic units of structure and function in living organisms.
  • All cells come from pre-existing cells.

This theory was developed through the work of scientists like Schleiden, Schwann, and Virchow.

2. Types of Cells

Cells fall into two major categories:

  • Prokaryotic Cells: Simple cells without a nucleus or membrane-bound organelles. Example: Bacteria.
  • Eukaryotic Cells: More complex cells with a nucleus and organelles. Example: Plant and animal cells.

3. Cell Organelles and Their Functions

Eukaryotic cells contain specialized structures called organelles, each with a specific function:

  • Nucleus: Contains DNA and controls cell activities.
  • Ribosomes: Synthesize proteins.
  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER):
    • Rough ER: Has ribosomes; helps modify and transport proteins.
    • Smooth ER: Makes lipids and detoxifies substances.
  • Golgi Apparatus: Packages and distributes proteins and lipids.
  • Mitochondria: Produces energy (ATP) through cellular respiration.
  • Chloroplasts: Site of photosynthesis in plant cells.
  • Lysosomes: Break down waste and cellular debris.
  • Vacuoles: Store water, nutrients, and waste. Large in plant cells.
  • Cell Membrane: Controls what enters and leaves the cell; helps maintain homeostasis.
  • Cell Wall: Rigid structure in plant, fungi, and some bacterial cells that provides support and protection.
  • Cytoplasm: Jelly-like fluid where organelles are suspended.
  • Cytoskeleton: Network of fibers for shape and movement.

4. Cell Membrane Structure

The cell membrane is made of a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins. It is selectively permeable, meaning it controls what substances can enter or leave the cell.

  • Phospholipids: Have hydrophilic heads and hydrophobic tails, forming the bilayer.
  • Proteins: Help with transport and communication.
  • Carbohydrates: Act as identification tags for the cell.

5. Transport Across the Membrane

Cells use different methods to move materials across the membrane:

  • Passive Transport: No energy required.
    • Diffusion: Movement of molecules from high to low concentration.
    • Osmosis: Diffusion of water.
    • Facilitated Diffusion: Use of protein channels to move molecules across.
  • Active Transport: Requires energy (ATP) to move molecules from low to high concentration.
  • Endocytosis: Cell takes in large materials by engulfing them.
  • Exocytosis: Cell releases large materials outside.

6. Homeostasis and the Cell

Cells must maintain homeostasis, a stable internal environment. They achieve this through membrane transport and responding to external stimuli. Disruption of homeostasis can lead to cell damage or death.

Summary

This unit explains the structure and function of cells, the differences between cell types, and the importance of organelles. Understanding how cells function and maintain balance is essential for exploring more complex biological systems.

Unit 4: Energy and Metabolism

All living organisms need energy to perform life processes such as growth, reproduction, and maintaining homeostasis. This unit explores how cells capture, store, and use energy through photosynthesis and cellular respiration.

1. What is Metabolism?

Metabolism refers to all the chemical reactions in an organism that maintain life. These reactions are divided into two main categories:

  • Anabolism: Builds larger molecules from smaller ones (e.g., photosynthesis).
  • Catabolism: Breaks down molecules to release energy (e.g., cellular respiration).

The energy released or stored in these reactions is in the form of ATP (adenosine triphosphate), the cell’s main energy currency.

2. ATP: Energy for the Cell

ATP stores and provides energy for many cellular processes. It consists of:

  • Adenine (a nitrogen base)
  • Ribose (a sugar)
  • Three phosphate groups

When a phosphate group is removed, ATP becomes ADP (adenosine diphosphate), and energy is released.

3. Photosynthesis

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.

Equation:

6CO2 + 6H2O + light → C6H12O6 + 6O2

  • Occurs in: Chloroplasts
  • Pigment: Chlorophyll absorbs light energy

Photosynthesis has two main stages:

  • Light-dependent reactions: Capture light and produce ATP and NADPH.
  • Calvin Cycle (light-independent reactions): Uses ATP and NADPH to fix carbon and build glucose.

4. Cellular Respiration

Cellular respiration breaks down glucose to release energy in the form of ATP.

Equation:

C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + ATP

  • Occurs in: Mitochondria

It occurs in three main steps:

  • Glycolysis: Glucose is broken into pyruvate in the cytoplasm. Produces a small amount of ATP.
  • Krebs Cycle: Occurs in the mitochondria. Produces carbon dioxide, NADH, and FADH2.
  • Electron Transport Chain: Uses NADH and FADH2 to produce most of the cell’s ATP.

5. Anaerobic Respiration (Fermentation)

When oxygen is not available, some cells perform anaerobic respiration (fermentation) to produce ATP.

  • Lactic Acid Fermentation: Occurs in muscle cells; produces lactic acid.
  • Alcoholic Fermentation: Occurs in yeast; produces alcohol and carbon dioxide.

Anaerobic respiration produces much less ATP than aerobic respiration.

Summary

This unit connects energy to life. Cells rely on ATP to power their functions, which they obtain from glucose through cellular respiration. In plants, glucose is produced through photosynthesis using light energy. Understanding these cycles shows how energy flows through ecosystems and powers all living things.

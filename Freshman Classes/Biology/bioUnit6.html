Unit 6

Unit 6: Genetics

Genetics is the study of heredity — how traits are passed from parents to offspring. In this unit, students explore the foundational principles of inheritance, the structure and function of DNA, and the role of genes in determining physical and behavioral characteristics.

1. Mendelian Genetics

Gregor Mendel, known as the "Father of Genetics," used pea plants to discover the basic principles of heredity. He identified dominant and recessive traits and formulated the Laws of Inheritance:

  • Law of Segregation: Each individual has two alleles for each gene, which separate during gamete formation. Each gamete receives one allele.
  • Law of Independent Assortment: Genes for different traits are inherited independently of each other.

Key terms include genotype (genetic makeup), phenotype (physical traits), homozygous (two identical alleles), and heterozygous (two different alleles).

2. Punnett Squares and Probability

Punnett squares are tools used to predict the probability of offspring inheriting particular traits. They help visualize genotype combinations and calculate ratios of expected phenotypes.

Students use Punnett squares for monohybrid (one trait) and dihybrid (two traits) crosses. These exercises reinforce understanding of dominant/recessive relationships and probability in genetics.

3. DNA and the Structure of Genes

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that contains the genetic instructions for living organisms. It is composed of two strands forming a double helix, made up of nucleotides:

  • Adenine (A)
  • Thymine (T)
  • Cytosine (C)
  • Guanine (G)

A pairs with T, and C pairs with G. Genes are specific sequences of DNA that code for proteins. Chromosomes are long strands of DNA, and humans typically have 23 pairs.

4. Inheritance Patterns Beyond Mendel

Not all traits follow Mendelian inheritance. This unit introduces:

  • Incomplete Dominance: A blend of traits (e.g., red + white = pink flowers).
  • Codominance: Both alleles are expressed equally (e.g., AB blood type).
  • Multiple Alleles: More than two allele options exist for a gene (e.g., blood types A, B, O).
  • Polygenic Traits: Traits controlled by many genes (e.g., skin color, height).

5. Mutations and Genetic Disorders

Mutations are changes in the DNA sequence. They can be:

  • Point Mutations: One base is substituted for another.
  • Frameshift Mutations: Insertions or deletions that alter the reading frame of a gene.

Some mutations are harmless, while others can lead to genetic disorders like cystic fibrosis or sickle cell anemia.

6. Genetic Technology

The unit concludes with modern applications of genetics, such as:

  • Genetic Engineering: Direct manipulation of an organism’s genes.
  • CRISPR: A precise gene-editing tool used in research and medicine.
  • DNA Fingerprinting: Identifying individuals based on their unique DNA sequences.
  • Gene Therapy: Treating genetic diseases by inserting healthy genes.

Summary

Genetics is a critical part of biology that connects all living things through heredity. By understanding genes, alleles, DNA, and inheritance patterns, students gain insight into both the predictability and complexity of life. This unit lays the groundwork for future exploration of molecular biology, biotechnology, and evolution.

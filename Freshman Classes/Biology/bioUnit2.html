Unit 2: Chemistry of Life

Understanding biology requires a foundation in basic chemistry because all living things are composed of matter and undergo chemical processes. This unit explores the structure of atoms, the nature of chemical bonds, the properties of water, and the role of biomolecules in life.

1. Matter and Atoms

All matter is made up of atoms, which are the smallest units of elements that retain their properties. An atom consists of:

  • Protons (positive charge) — found in the nucleus
  • Neutrons (no charge) — also in the nucleus
  • Electrons (negative charge) — orbit the nucleus

The atomic number equals the number of protons. Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons are called isotopes.

2. Chemical Bonds

Atoms form chemical bonds to achieve stability. There are three main types of bonds:

  • Ionic Bonds: Electrons are transferred from one atom to another, creating charged ions that attract each other (e.g., NaCl).
  • Covalent Bonds: Electrons are shared between atoms (e.g., H2O).
  • Hydrogen Bonds: Weak bonds between polar molecules, especially important in water and DNA structure.

3. Properties of Water

Water is essential for life and has unique properties due to its polarity:

  • Cohesion: Water molecules stick to each other.
  • Adhesion: Water molecules stick to other surfaces.
  • High specific heat: Water resists temperature changes, helping to regulate climate and body temperature.
  • Solvent properties: Water dissolves many substances, making it the “universal solvent.”
  • Ice density: Ice floats because it's less dense than liquid water, insulating aquatic life in winter.

4. Acids, Bases, and pH

The pH scale measures how acidic or basic a solution is:

  • Acids: pH less than 7; produce H+ ions in solution.
  • Bases: pH greater than 7; produce OH− ions.
  • Neutral: pH of 7; pure water.

Living organisms use buffers to maintain stable pH levels, essential for enzyme activity and cellular function.

5. Macromolecules of Life

Life relies on four main types of carbon-based macromolecules:

  • Carbohydrates: Provide energy and structure. Made of sugars (monosaccharides). Example: glucose, starch, cellulose.
  • Lipids: Store energy and make up cell membranes. Includes fats, oils, and waxes.
  • Proteins: Made of amino acids; used for structure, enzymes, transport, and signaling.
  • Nucleic Acids: DNA and RNA, which store and transmit genetic information. Made of nucleotides.

6. Enzymes and Biochemical Reactions

Enzymes are proteins that speed up chemical reactions by lowering activation energy. They are highly specific to the substrates they act on.

  • Active site: The region where the enzyme binds to the substrate.
  • Enzyme-substrate complex: Temporary binding that facilitates the reaction.

Enzyme activity is affected by temperature, pH, and substrate concentration. Enzymes are essential for digestion, energy production, and more.

Summary

This unit introduces the chemical principles underlying all biological processes. By understanding atoms, bonding, water, pH, macromolecules, and enzymes, students gain insight into the molecular building blocks of life and the reactions that sustain it.

Unit 5: Cell Division

Cell division is the process by which cells reproduce and make copies of themselves. It plays a critical role in growth, development, repair, and reproduction. This unit explores the cell cycle, mitosis, and meiosis in detail.

1. The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is the series of events that cells go through as they grow and divide. It is made up of interphase and the mitotic phase (M phase).

  • G1 Phase: Cell grows and performs normal functions.
  • S Phase: DNA is replicated (copied).
  • G2 Phase: Cell prepares for division by producing proteins and organelles.
  • M Phase: Includes mitosis and cytokinesis (actual cell division).

2. Mitosis

Mitosis is the process by which a cell divides its nucleus and produces two genetically identical daughter cells. It is used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.

  • Prophase: Chromosomes condense and become visible; spindle fibers form; nuclear envelope breaks down.
  • Metaphase: Chromosomes line up in the middle of the cell.
  • Anaphase: Sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite ends of the cell.
  • Telophase: Nuclear envelopes reform; chromosomes uncoil.

After mitosis, cytokinesis occurs, splitting the cytoplasm and forming two separate cells.

3. Regulation of the Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is tightly controlled by regulatory proteins and checkpoints. If regulation fails, it can lead to uncontrolled cell division, also known as cancer.

  • Checkpoints: Occur at G1, G2, and M phases to ensure proper division.
  • Apoptosis: Programmed cell death that occurs if cells are damaged beyond repair.

4. Meiosis

Meiosis is a type of cell division that produces four genetically different gametes (sperm or egg cells) with half the number of chromosomes. It is essential for sexual reproduction.

  • Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes separate.
  • Meiosis II: Sister chromatids separate (similar to mitosis).

Key differences from mitosis include:

  • Crossing Over: In Prophase I, homologous chromosomes exchange genetic material, increasing genetic variation.
  • Independent Assortment: Random distribution of chromosomes increases diversity.
  • Result: Four haploid cells that are genetically unique.

5. Comparing Mitosis and Meiosis

Feature Mitosis Meiosis
Purpose Growth, repair, asexual reproduction Sexual reproduction
# of Divisions 1 2
# of Cells Produced 2 4
Genetic Identity Identical Unique
Chromosome Number Diploid (2n) Haploid (n)

Summary

Cell division allows organisms to grow, repair damage, and reproduce. Mitosis ensures genetic consistency in body cells, while meiosis introduces genetic diversity for sexual reproduction. Proper regulation of the cell cycle is essential to prevent diseases like cancer.
